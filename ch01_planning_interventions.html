Introduction: Why Behavior Change?

Population-level behavior change research has become especially topical because patterns of behavior shared across populations are key determinants of health and well-being. Decades of epidemiological research demonstrate a clear link between everyday lifestyle behaviors and long-term health outcomes.

📊 The Alameda County Study (Belloc & Breslow, 1972)

Followed 7,000 people over 10 years and showed that sleep, exercise, alcohol consumption, and eating habits significantly predicted mortality — among the earliest studies to empirically connect everyday behaviors to life expectancy.

📉 Mortality Risk (Kvaavik et al., 2010)

Among 4,886 individuals, those who smoked, consumed fewer than 3 fruit/vegetable portions daily, did less than 2 h physical activity/week, and drank more than 14 alcohol units had an all-cause mortality risk equivalent to being 12 years older.

🌍 The Scale of the Problem: In England, more than 60% of the population is overweight or obese, associated with type 2 diabetes, coronary heart disease, hypertension, osteoarthritis, and cancer — costing an estimated £5 billion annually (Department of Health, 2013). Engaging people in self-management and behavior change is critical to health service sustainability.

Despite the clear motivation for change, understanding how to bring about that change is far more complex. A review by Michie et al. (2008) identified at least 137 individual behavior change techniques, concluding that a "one-size-fits-all" approach cannot be adopted — techniques must be carefully matched to the specific antecedents of the target behavior and the target population.

Change is Possible: The Neurological Foundation

Brain Plasticity as the Basis for Change

The human brain — approximately 86 billion neurons — has the capacity, over time, to rewrite the processes that direct what we think, feel, and do (Maslin & Christensen, 2007; Herculano-Houzel, 2009). Activities that once seem difficult or strange can, through practice, become automatic and everyday.

The challenge for behavior change scientists is to model, explain, and then harness the cognitive, emotional, and behavioral dynamism of our regulatory control systems (Reynolds & Branscombe, 2015) — and to develop interventions that help people accelerate and consolidate behavior change processes.

✅ Evidence That Change Works: Reductions in smoking and unsafe sexual behavior; increases in physical activity, healthy diets, self-care, and health screening have all been observed following specific interventions. A NICE-commissioned review of 103 systematic reviews found that overall, interventions were successful in changing behavior patterns (Jepson et al., 2010; Johnson et al., 2010).

However, effectiveness is not guaranteed. Careful modeling of modifiable regulatory processes (behavioral determinants) and matched selection of proven change techniques is a prerequisite to success. Poorly designed interventions may not only be ineffective — they may produce negative effects.

Changing Individuals vs. Changing Environments

Understanding how to help people reprogram themselves is important, but environmental changes may provide more powerful levers of change. Changes in the physical, commercial, and social environment can prompt widespread behavior change without requiring persuasion of individuals.

👤

Individual-Level Approaches

Target intrapersonal regulatory processes or psychological "determinants" — changing what people believe, feel, and are motivated to do. If effective and widely applied, individual change can have population-wide effects.

Attitude change Self-efficacy Skill training Planning
🏙️

Environmental / Structural Approaches

Target the context in which choices are made — changing the physical, commercial, legal, or social environment rather than individual cognitions.

Food taxes Default systems Legislation Urban design

⚠️ Whole-System Thinking Required: For complex problems like obesity prevention, individual approaches alone are insufficient. A whole-system approach targeting multiple processes simultaneously is needed. Organizational change matters too: Haslam et al. (2014) found that residents who redesigned their own care home environment showed significantly greater cognitive improvements than those whose environment was redesigned for them.

Legislative Change

Legislation can have profound behavioral effects. The UK smoking ban in public places (2007) was followed by significant reductions in myocardial infarction hospital admissions. A parallel US study (Sargent et al., 2004) found a significant drop during a 6-month smoking ban in Montana, while surrounding areas without a ban experienced a non-significant increase.

Policy Frameworks: NUDGE & EAST

Both the UK and US governments use accessible frameworks to guide behavior change policy. These approaches favor low-friction design — making desired behaviors easier without banning alternatives.

The NUDGE Framework (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008)

A NUDGE alters choice architecture "without forbidding options or significantly changing economic incentives" — changes that are "easy and cheap to avoid." Putting fruit at eye level counts as a nudge; banning junk food does not.

💡 The Default Effect (Organ Donation): Countries with opt-out (assumed consent) systems show donation rates above 99%; countries like the UK using opt-in systems show only ~17% (Johnson & Goldstein, 2003). Shepherd et al. (2014) confirmed that opt-out countries have significantly higher total transplants. This illustrates the enormous behavioral power of default architecture.

💡 Financial Incentives: Pregnant women paid to abstain from smoking showed 24% cessation at 12-week postpartum follow-up vs. only 3% in the control group (Heil et al., 2008). Key question: can incentive-based interventions generate intrinsic motivation that sustains change after incentives are withdrawn?

Intervention Mapping (IM): Systematic Planning

Effective behavior change interventions require careful, systematic design. The Intervention Mapping (IM) approach (Bartholomew et al., 2011; interventionmapping.com) provides a comprehensive and practical guide, ensuring that change techniques are evidence-based and precisely matched to the regulatory processes driving the target behavior.

📌 Why IM Matters: Without systematic planning, interventions may fail to incorporate the detailed tailoring on which effectiveness depends. "Bland, off-the-peg interventions" lack the versatility to match techniques to specific behavior change challenges — and so fail to progress a science of behavior change.

The 6 Stages of Intervention Mapping

  1. Needs Assessment Identify the problem caused by specified behavior patterns through literature reviews and stakeholder consultation. A thorough needs assessment can prevent wasting time on unnecessary or ineffectual interventions — it may reveal that dissemination of best practice, rather than a new intervention, is the optimal response.
  2. Defining Intervention Objectives Precisely define the behavior changes required. For example, a sexual health intervention might target condom acquisition, carrying, negotiation, and correct use — each a distinct behavioral target requiring separate analysis (corresponding to Characteristic 1 in Table 1.1).
  3. Identifying Regulatory Processes & Change Techniques Identify which modifiable mechanisms maintain the current (unwanted) behavior, then select evidence-based change techniques capable of altering those mechanisms. If designers make incorrect assumptions about key behavioral drivers, they will employ suboptimal or even counterproductive techniques (e.g., using fear appeals with an already-motivated audience).
  4. Assembling the Intervention Develop practical delivery formats and assemble the intervention components. Decisions include delivery mode (face-to-face, digital, print), setting (school, worksite, community), and the fit between components and the cultural/practical context.
  5. Planning Delivery & Implementation in Context Anticipate how the intervention will be used in everyday settings. Is it engaging, acceptable, practical, and sustainable? Pilot testing and co-creation with recipients is critical. Competent delivery may require additional training — e.g., a review of motivational interviewing training found only 2 of 11 studies reported 75%+ clinician proficiency (Hall et al., 2015).
  6. Planning Intervention Evaluation Define outcome, process, and economic evaluation measures from the outset. When behavior changes are defined in Stage 2, simultaneously identify how to measure those changes. When regulatory mechanisms are identified in Stage 3, select measures to assess the change processes by which the intervention is hypothesized to work.

Choice or Habit? Dual-Process Models of Behavior

Behavior change is often framed as helping people make the right choices — implying deliberate, rational decision-making. Yet many problematic behavior patterns are enacted without conscious deliberation. They are automatic and habitual, triggered by environmental cues. Changing the context may be more effective than changing the mind.

Dual-Process Theory: Two Regulatory Systems

A series of dual-process models (Borland, 2014; Kahneman, 2011; Strack & Deutsch, 2004) characterize two distinct regulatory systems operating in parallel on a moment-to-moment basis — one conscious and deliberative, the other automatic and impulsive.

🧠 Reflective System

  • Conscious, deliberative control of action
  • Rational weighing of pros and cons
  • Goal-directed and effortful processing
  • Susceptible to cognitive depletion (ego depletion; Baumeister et al., 1998)
  • Responds to verbal instructions & planning prompts
  • Can consciously monitor and modify routine behavior

⚡ Impulsive System

  • Automatic, habitual, fast responses
  • Operates with low conscious awareness
  • Triggered by environmental cues via conditioning
  • Built through repetition (operant conditioning)
  • Difficult to modify through willpower alone
  • Produces subjective "urges" and "cravings"

The Reflective-Impulsive Model (RIM; Strack & Deutsch, 2004)

The RIM, elaborated by Borland (2014) as the Context, Executive and Operational Systems (CEOS) model, proposes that the two systems operate in parallel. The reflective system can become aware of the impulsive system's operation — perceiving "urges" and "cravings" — and this awareness is critical to regaining reflective control over otherwise automatic behavior patterns.

⚠️ Mindless Eating (Wansink, 2007): Eating is rarely consciously monitored — each eating episode can be initiated by internal impulses or external cues with little deliberation. A highly motivated person may still find themselves consuming more calories than intended, because the impulsive system governs action independently of reflective intentions.

💡 Practical Application — Self-Monitoring: A food diary completed immediately after eating deliberately activates the reflective system, disrupting mindless consumption. This illustrates how a change technique can be selected through understanding the underlying regulatory processes (Abraham & Michie, 2008). However, conscious self-monitoring is cognitively taxing. Sustainable change ultimately requires making new behavioral routines automatic — breaking unwanted habits and building new ones (Dean, 2013).

The IMB Skills Model: Targeting Reflective Processes

The Information-Motivation-Behavioral Skills (IMB) model (Fisher & Fisher, 1992) is a well-tested framework for designing individual-level interventions targeting the reflective system. The model proposes that changes in the reflective system occur when three conditions are met:

📖

Information

Relevant, accurate knowledge about behavior and its consequences. Critical when motivated, skilled people lack awareness of health risks or behavioral options (e.g., calorie content, STI transmission routes).

🔥

Motivation

The willingness and intent to perform the behavior. Information provision alone is insufficient for unmotivated audiences — motivational processes must be directly targeted through specific techniques.

🛠️

Behavioral Skills

The practical capabilities to execute and sustain the behavior. Motivated, informed people can still fail to change if they lack skills to translate intention into action or to manage the social challenges of change.

Five Key Determinants of Motivation (Fishbein et al., 2001)

  1. Outcome ExpectanciesBelief that the advantages of changing behavior outweigh the disadvantages
  2. Anticipated Positive AffectExpectation that behavior change will lead to a positive emotional reaction (pride, satisfaction)
  3. Social NormsBelief that significant others will approve of the proposed change
  4. Identity / Self-ImageBelief that the behavior change is consistent with who the person is or aspires to be
  5. Self-Efficacy (Bandura, 1997)Belief that one is capable of successfully performing the required behavior. High self-efficacy leads to more challenging goals, more effort, more flexible problem-solving, and greater persistence.

Three Classes of Behavioral Skills

🎯

Self-Regulatory Skills

Goal-setting, self-monitoring, and planning. Based on Control Theory (Carver & Scheier, 1982): set goals → monitor current behavior → take action → monitor progress → re-set goals. Includes SMART goal-setting (Doran, 1981).

💪

Motor Skills

Physical capabilities required for the behavior (e.g., correct inhaler use, handwashing technique, safe injection practice). May require modeling, guided practice, and feedback on performance.

🤝

Social Skills

Interpersonal capabilities to negotiate change with others (condom negotiation, refusal of alcohol, assertiveness). Role play with modeling and video feedback is highly effective (Video Interaction Guidance; Kennedy et al., 2011).

Changing Impulsive Processes: Breaking & Making Habits

Skinner's (1938) work on operant conditioning systematically explained how environments shape automatic behavior: stimuli associated with rewarding experiences become cues that automatically trigger approach responses, building complex impulsively regulated behavior patterns over time.

🔄 Inhibition Training (Go/No-Go Paradigm)

Pictures of desired stimuli (e.g., unhealthy foods) are repeatedly paired with "stop" signals. Over many pairings, the established impulse to approach becomes inhibited — making it easier to resist automatic approach responses in real-world settings.

A meta-analysis of 19 experiments (Allom et al., 2015) suggests inhibition training may effectively change health behavior patterns.

📋 If-Then Planning (Implementation Intentions)

Formulating "if situation X, then I will do Y" plans. Gollwitzer & Sheeran (2006) show these help motivated people translate intentions into action.

Example: Adding one if-then planning session to a weight-loss program led participants to lose twice as much weight over 2 months (Luszczynska et al., 2007). Mediation analysis confirmed the hypothesized mechanism.

Why If-Then Planning Bridges Both Systems

The plan is formulated using the reflective system, but the pairing of a cue ("if") with an imagined response ("then") has the capacity to alter the impulsive system — so that when the cue is encountered later, a new automatic response is prompted without requiring conscious deliberation at that moment.

Habit Reversal & Food Rehabilitation

Habit Reversal Therapy (Dean, 2013)

Originally for Tourette's syndrome — intensive (10+ h of contact): (1) build awareness of unwanted behavioral tics and their triggers, (2) practice a competing behavior whenever the urge arises. McGuire et al. (2014) confirm effectiveness of this intensive approach.

Food Rehabilitation (Kessler, 2009)

Parallels habit reversal for unhealthy eating: (1) identify environmental cues and "premonitionary urges," (2) specify competing behaviors (e.g., taking a different route past a bakery), (3) cognitive-emotional reconstruction — reshaping how food cues are mentally represented to reduce their pull.

Detailed Tailoring: The Same Technique, Different Applications

Even within one technique category, precise tailoring to the specific barrier is critical:

Sheeran & Orbell (2000): A planning prompt asking women to specify when and where to make a cervical screening appointment increased attendance from 67% → 92%. The barrier was failure to initiate action — so action planning worked.

Sheeran et al. (2007): Advised anxious psychotherapy patients to use self-talk when appointment worries arose. Attendance rose from 63% → 75%. The barrier here was cognitive elaboration of anxiety — so a thought-regulation technique was needed instead.

Evaluation of Behavior Change Interventions

The final stage of IM is evaluation planning. Although listed last, it must be anticipated from the outset. When intervention objectives are defined in Stage 2, designers should simultaneously identify how to measure them. Three broad evaluation types are distinguished:

📊

Outcome Evaluation

Did the intervention change the target behavior? May include both behavioral measures (e.g., physical activity, smoking) and health outcomes (e.g., weight, disease incidence).

🔬

Process Evaluation

How did the intervention work? Did it change the targeted mechanisms (e.g., attitudes, self-efficacy, planning)? Mediation analyses test the intervention's logic model (Moore et al., 2014).

💰

Economic Evaluation

How much does the intervention cost per unit of effectiveness? Preventive interventions are often highly cost-effective — e.g., HIV prevention vs. lifetime US treatment cost of ~$240,000+.

The RE-AIM Framework (Glasgow et al., 2002; Green & Glasgow, 2006)

A comprehensive model for evaluating real-world delivery and implementation impact:

R
Reach
How many of the target population participated, and how representative were they?
E
Effectiveness
Range of effects, including intended outcomes, quality of life, and unintended consequences
A
Adoption
Were end-users (nurses, teachers, managers, public) persuaded to use the intervention? Depends on ease, value, cost, and goal compatibility
I
Implementation
Was it delivered as designed? Fidelity of delivery — did recipients receive adequate "dose"?
M
Maintenance
Is the intervention sustained in real-world settings long-term? Can the organization resource ongoing delivery?

✅ Key Insight: Even the most efficacious intervention has no public health impact if it is not adopted and translated into practice (Rogers, 2003). Understanding adoption and diffusion processes is as critical as the intervention design itself.

Conclusions

Towards a Science of Behavior Change

We know a great deal about behavior change intervention design. By carefully following systematic planning procedures — understanding the detail of regulatory processes, tailoring techniques precisely, and rigorously evaluating both outcomes and processes — we can optimize effectiveness. Simplified "off-the-peg" approaches may sometimes generate results but lack the versatility to match techniques to specific challenges. Behavior change science advances project by project through careful planning and rigorous process evaluation.
